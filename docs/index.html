Methodology

Gender assignment

Around 77% of candidates had no gender recorded in the Democracy Club source data. Gender was predicted algorithmically in three stages:

  1. gender_guesser library — an open-source name database covering common first names internationally. Applied first, produces “high” or “medium” confidence results.
  2. ONS historical baby names dataset (1904–2024) — used for names not resolved by stage 1, and where a birth year was available. Year-aware lookup accounts for names whose gender balance has shifted over time (e.g. “Ashley”, “Kim”).
  3. Claude Sonnet 4.6 (AI) — used for the remaining 4,059 names that could not be resolved by stages 1 or 2. The AI was asked to classify first names by likely gender based on cultural and linguistic context.

63 candidates (<0.3%) remain unclassified — primarily names where gender could not be determined with confidence. All gender percentages are calculated only among candidates with a known gender.

Gender is treated as binary (male/female) for prediction purposes. This does not reflect the full spectrum of gender identity and should not be treated as a candidate’s self-identified gender.

Incumbency

Incumbent status indicates whether a 2026 candidate was a sitting councillor in the same ward and council as of 2025. A candidate is classified as an incumbent if and only if all three conditions are met:

  1. Council match — the candidate’s 2026 council (from Democracy Club data) is matched to a council entry in the 2025 sitting councillor data (from opencouncildata.co.uk, scraped May 2026). Council names are normalised by stripping prefixes (“London Borough of”, “Royal Borough of”) and suffixes (“County Council”, “Borough Council” etc.) before matching.
  2. Ward match — the candidate’s 2026 ward name (from Democracy Club’s post_label field) is matched against the ward names in the 2025 data. Exact match is tried first; if that fails, fuzzy matching (difflib.get_close_matches with cutoff 0.6) is attempted, but only where the first word of both ward names matches — this prevents cross-area false positives caused by ward boundary reorganisations. Ward matches that required fuzzy lookup are logged for review.
  3. Name match — the candidate’s official SOPN name is compared against the names of sitting councillors in the matched ward using difflib.SequenceMatcher. A match is accepted if the ratio is ≥ 0.80. This threshold handles middle names present in SOPN data but absent from the 2025 councillor records (e.g. “John Robert Smith” matching “John Smith”) and minor spelling differences.

Coverage and limitations

  • 316 councils scraped from opencouncildata.co.uk (deduped by name). 156 councils in the Democracy Club 2026 data are matched against these.
  • 3.4% of candidates are in councils with no 2025 data (primarily East Surrey and West Surrey, which are newly created councils for 2026 and have no 2025 sitting councillors).
  • 6.0% of candidates are in wards where no ward match could be found — typically due to ward boundary reorganisations creating entirely new ward names.
  • Incumbents who chose not to re-stand are not counted. The “Defeated” figure only covers incumbents who stood in 2026 and lost their seat. We cannot reliably distinguish a councillor who retired from one whose seat was simply not up for election in 2026 (in multi-member wards, seats rotate).

Election type (full vs partial)

English councils use one of three election cycles:

  • Whole council (Full) — all seats elected at once, every four years. 222 of 316 councils in England use this model.
  • By thirds (Partial) — roughly one third of seats contested each year for three consecutive years, then a fallow year. 87 councils use this model.
  • By halves (Partial) — half of seats contested in alternate years. 7 councils use this model (typically those with two councillors per ward).

Election type for each council is read directly from the Election Model metadata field in the opencouncildata.co.uk dataset. Of the 156 councils in this dataset, 152 were matched; the 4 unmatched councils (East Surrey, West Surrey, Newport City, Powys) are newly created or Welsh authorities not present in the source data and are shown as “Unknown”.

This classification affects interpretation: a council with 54 seats electing 18 in 2026 (partial, by thirds) is not inherently less female-friendly than one electing all 54 — different seats were at stake.

Known limitations

  • Gender is predicted from names and is not self-reported. It is treated as binary for analysis purposes only.
  • Predictions are imperfect; the confidence field (High/Medium/Low) indicates reliability. Areas with many Low-confidence predictions have less reliable gender balance figures.
  • Some councils (county councils) do not map to a single LAD boundary and are excluded from the choropleth map but included in all other statistics.
  • Incumbent matching cannot identify incumbents in newly created or renamed councils/wards. Ward boundary reorganisations (several councils underwent changes for 2026) mean some incumbents are undetectable.
  • Re-election rates and new councillor counts reflect only contested elections in this dataset. Uncontested returns and by-elections after the main election are not included.
